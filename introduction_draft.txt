INTRODUCTION — REVISED DRAFT
================================================================================

Global dairy demand is projected to grow substantially as the world population
approaches 9.7 billion by 2050 (United Nations Department of Economic and Social
Affairs, 2022), placing intensifying pressure on producers to improve both
productivity and environmental performance simultaneously. Feed costs represent
the single largest operating expense on commercial dairy farms, typically
accounting for 50–60% of total production costs, and diet composition directly
determines animal performance and farm profitability (Buza et al., 2014). Among
the variables available to nutritionists when formulating rations, the
forage-to-concentrate ratio (F:C) is one of the most practically accessible and
economically influential levers, as it governs diet energy density, rumen
fermentation pattern, and voluntary feed intake simultaneously.

High-forage diets provide the structural carbohydrates required for normal rumen
function, but their elevated neutral detergent fiber (NDF) content imposes a
physical fill constraint that limits voluntary dry matter intake (DMI) and dilutes
diet metabolizable energy concentration (Allen, 2000). Increasing concentrate
inclusion attenuates this limitation, raising energy density and improving gross
feed efficiency (GFE; energy-corrected milk per unit of DMI) when energy supply
is the primary constraint on milk production (Connor, 2015). However, excessive
concentrate inclusion risks subacute ruminal acidosis, milk fat depression, and
associated metabolic disorders that can offset these production benefits (Krause
and Oetzel, 2006; Zebeli et al., 2008).

Beyond productivity, the F:C ratio is also a key determinant of enteric methane
(CH4) emissions, which represent a substantial fraction of total greenhouse gas
output from dairy systems. Forage-rich diets promote acetate-type ruminal
fermentation, generating a hydrogen surplus captured predominantly as CH4, such
that methane yield per unit of energy-corrected milk tends to increase with
forage inclusion (Boadi et al., 2004; Beauchemin et al., 2008). Higher
concentrate inclusion promotes propionate-pathway fermentation, reducing hydrogen
availability for methanogenesis and lowering methane intensity per unit of milk,
though the net effect on absolute methane output per animal depends on the
simultaneous response of DMI and milk production (Benchaar et al., 2001;
Aguerre et al., 2011).

While these general relationships between F:C ratio, production performance, and
emissions are well established in the research literature, how they manifest on
any given commercial farm depends on that farm's specific herd genetics,
management system, feed composition, and production goals. The practical
consequences of shifting dietary F:C — whether toward a high-forage or
low-forage strategy — are therefore farm-specific and cannot be reliably inferred
from population-level research trends alone. Evaluating such dietary scenarios
empirically on commercial farms is resource-intensive and rarely captures the
full complexity of real herd dynamics, feed storage losses, and manure management
interactions. Whole-farm simulation provides a practical complementary approach
by allowing farm-specific what-if scenarios to be tested systematically in silico.
The Ruminant Farm Systems (RuFaS) model integrates animal, feed, and manure
modules to simultaneously project production and environmental outcomes under
user-defined dietary scenarios, with a dynamic ration optimizer that adjusts
ingredient inclusion to meet biological constraints, making it well-suited for
this type of farm-specific dietary analysis (Gong et al., 2025; Reed et al.,
2025). Therefore, the objective of this study was to use the RuFaS model to
quantify the effects of a high-forage diet (HFD) versus a low-forage diet (LFD)
on production performance and environmental footprint for a single commercial
dairy farm.
