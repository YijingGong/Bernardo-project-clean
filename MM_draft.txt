Materials and methods
This study was carried out using the RuFaS, the data used is from a Minnesota farm, with the herd profile comprising Holstein cows, having 489 lactating cows, 24 springers, 401 heifers, and 39 calves. For the lactating cows' diet, a mixture of corn silage, ground dry corn, distillers' soluble, mid-maturity legume silage, 48% crude protein (CP) soybean meal, and an own farm mineral mix.
For measuring feed efficiency, several factors were considered, such as gross feed efficiency (GFE), dry matter intake (DMI), milk yield, manure nitrogen, manure volatile solids and non-volatile solids, feed emission, enteric methane, and total greenhouse gases (GHG) emission corrected to carbon. The milk yield and milk solids percentages were kept constant. GFE, as described by Gong et al. (2025), was calculated by using DMI, fat and protein corrected milk (FPCM 3.9% fat, 3.2% CP) in the following equation: 
GFE=FPCM/DMI, 
where the FPCM was calculated as: 
FPCM(kg\/yr)=milkproduction(kg\/yr)×0.327+milkfatyield(kg\/yr)×12.95+milkproteinyield(kg\/yr)×7.2
DMI was also measured in kg/yr.
Diet selection
Following the classification by Musco et al. (2020), two dietary treatments were established based on dry matter (DM) proportions: a high forage diet (HFD; 70:30 forage to concentrate) and a low forage diet (LFD; 55:45 forage to concentrate), utilizing the farms' already existing ingredients. With that, the NASEM Dairy 8 tool (NASEM, 2021) was selected to evaluate the farm’s feed in its F:C, DM, CP, Fats, NDF, Starch, and minerals. With that, we were able to conclude that the pre-existent feed had 73.2% of its DM constituted by forage, meaning that it is high in forage. And so, to formulate a Low Forage Diet, also utilizing the NASEM Dairy 8 tool, which had a 55.1% of its DM constituted by forage (Table 1), having slight and expected nutrient  composition differences (Aguerre et al. 2011).

RuFaS inputs 
Following the definition of both high and low forage diets from Musco, the feed inclusion (Table 2) was inputted on the RuFaS file responsible for the feed formulation. For this study, the model's auto-optimizer was not utilized. Instead, the feed optimizer was set to a tolerance of 0.1, allowing the RuFaS model to adjust the initial inclusion rate of each feed ingredient by a maximum of 10% to meet the cows' dietary needs. Accounting for the stochastic nature of RuFaS, each feed scenario had four runs, and only the last four years of data from the 10-year simulation were used; therefore, the averages of these runs are evaluated.
Because the milk production, milk protein, and fat percentages are also user-inputted, and we don't have information regarding the DMI of the cows from the existing farm, it is to be expected that RuFaS will make slight changes to the feed profile and total DMI.

RuFaS Output Filters and Study Parameters
To evaluate the simulated scenarios, several performance and environmental variables were tracked:
Production and intake: Milk yield (kg/cow/d), lactating cow DMI kg/d, feed efficiency, and (GFE).
Manure composition: Manure nitrogen, total ammoniacal nitrogen (TAN), and volatile solids (all measured in kg/cow/d).
Emissions and indicators: Enteric methane (g/cow/d), total GHG emissions (kg CO₂-eq/cow/d), GHG intensity (total GHG/milk yield), and GHG yield (total GHG/DMI).
The diets generated by RuFaS were evaluated based on inclusion rate, total weight, and nutrient profile. Additionally, the two scenarios were compared regarding their total CO₂-eq emissions, which were partitioned into feed production, manure management, and enteric methane sources.
Data were extracted using specific simulation filters. Simulation logs were also monitored to verify successful diet formulation and to serve as a quality control measure for the generated diets.
Data Processing and Evaluation Strategy
RuFaS outputs for each diet were averaged across four independent simulation runs. The emission profiles, inclusion rates, and diet profiles of the two scenarios were then compared against each other and evaluated alongside outputs from NASEM Dairy 8 (NASEM, 2021).
Because RuFaS dynamically optimizes diets based on predefined biological constraints (Hansen et al., 2021), inclusion rates between the two simulated diets were expected to vary by up to 10%. Consequently, the evaluation of inclusion rates was designed to specifically track how the model altered the overall proportions of concentrates versus forages to achieve optimal formulation.
To evaluate the model's adherence to these biological constraints, an output filter was applied to the simulation data. This filter was configured to identify specific simulation days where the model failed to reach a nutritional threshold, recording the frequency of these failures and the specific dietary constraint involved.
